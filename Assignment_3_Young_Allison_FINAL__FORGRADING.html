Introduction¶

For this problem you will derive, implement through gradient descent, and test the performance of a logistic regression classifier for a binary classification problem.

In this case, we'll assume our logistic regression problem will be applied to a two dimensional feature space. Our logistic regression model is:

$$f(\mathbf{x}_i,\mathbf{w})=\sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i)$$

where the sigmoid function is defined as $\sigma(x) = \frac{e^x}{1+e^x}$. Also, since this is a two-dimensional problem, we define $\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i = w_0 x_{i,0} + w_1 x_{i,1} + w_2 x_{i,2}$ and here, $x_{i,0} \triangleq 1$

As in class, we will interpret the response of the logistic regression classifier to be the likelihood of the data given the model. For one sample, $(y_i, \mathbf{x_i})$, this is given as:

$$P(Y=y_i|X=x_i) = f(\mathbf{x}_i,\mathbf{w})=\sigma(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i)$$

$$ - \sum_{n=1}^N [(1-y_i) (-wx_i)- log(1+e^{-wx_i})] $$

C. Gradient of the Cost Function¶

$$ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \frac{\Delta C (w)}{w_0} = x_0 \Big[(1-y_i) - \frac{e^{-wx_0}}{1 + e^{-wx_0}}\Big] \\\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \frac{\Delta C (w)}{w_1} = x_1 \Big[(1-y_i) - \frac{e^{-wx_1}}{1 + e^{-wx_1}}\Big] \ \\\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \frac{\Delta C (w)}{w_2} = x_2 \Big[(1-y_i) - \frac{e^{-wx_2}}{1 + e^{-wx_2}}\Big] \ $$

Summary from adjusting Parameters¶

Weight Vector (w)¶

Changes in the weight vector (created by random in this example), primarily impact how long it takes for the function to converge. The weights simply determined the starting point for the gradient descent process (as they determined the starting cost value). From there, they follow the gradient descent function until they converge (as defined by the change threshold).

In summary, the weight vector does not greatly influence outcome; it will be very close to the same value, no matter what the original weights are. The weight vector predominately impacts the time it takes to run the function and the number of iterations it takes to get to the determined "change" threshold.

Learning Rate (ETA= .4)¶

The learning rate alters the rate at which the function changes with each epoch. In a sense, this represents the "Step" change in x, as the slope of the derivative approaches zero. A higher learning rate means the function will take larger steps. Each iteration of the gradient descent will move by the learning rate times the update in the weights, divided by the number of observations.

Therefore, when the "steps" are really large, they may encompass the minimum at zero. When this happens, the function will never converge, because the step continually passes from one side of the minimum to the other, changing the direction of descent, and failing to ultimately progress closer to the minimum. On the otherhand, a lower learning rate means the function will take smaller steps. The smaller the step, the longer the function will take, but the likelihood of reaching a minimum cost that is closer to zero is greater. The smaller step allows the descent to continue in the same direction over multiple iterations, continuing down, rather than skipping back and forth.

Change Threshold (change = 5)¶

The change threshold determines the point at which the gradient descent function stops running and returns a final value for the weights vector. With each iteration of the gradient descent, the L2 norm of the difference between the previous weights and the new weights represents the new "change" value. This lets the function know how much the weights are changing between each iteration of the gradient descent function. You set the threshold to a point that is reasonable for convention. In this case I used .001. A smaller number is best, as it brings you closest to 0.

Summary of Selection Process¶

To determine the set of parameters on which I finally settled, I started with what seemed to be a reasonable number for "change" given the range of the data in the original data exploration (meaning it wouldn't be higher than the range of the data, nor drastically smaller than a difference between observations. I then isolated the learning rate to experiment with. Using a series of trials, I settled on a general minimum of cost on the training data (other parameters held constant), at the learning rate of .4. I then checked my assumptions on the change threshold, and confirmed it had little influence.

Finally, I used a chage starting point of 5, learning rate of .4, and used a conventional change threshold of 001 to then observe changes in the values of the randomly assigned weight vector. Ultimately, the randomly change weights did not influence the final cost value by much. The weights simply determined the starting point for the gradient descent process (as they determined the starting cost value). From there, the gradient descent function iterated until it converged on a point (as defined by the change threshold), which was similar for each w vector.

M. Why Cross Validation?¶

Cross validation is a supervised learning technique used to prevent the occurance of overfitting in a model. To use cross validation, you divide you orginal labeled dataset into two (or more) divisions; holding out at subset to test your model on unseen data. If you use all of the data you have, you may get a perfect prediction of that data, but will not have any way to determine how your model will perform on new, unseen data. By keeping some data separated from the training of your model, you can test the model in a "new" situation.

A. Do young hearts benefit equally from machine learning algorithms?¶

As one ages, there are few things scarier than thinking of the diseases you may or may not acquire. Some, like cancer, can seem so mysterious and stealthy- sneaking in the shadows to prey on anyone foolish enough to feel safe and comfortable. Other are more steady and methodical killers; they build little by little, often undetected. Heart disease is one of these slow and steady killers.

And so how does one know when they "have heart disease." It turns out, it is not exactly a simple definition.

According to the American Heart Association, "Heart and blood vessel disease (also called heart disease) includes numerous problems, many of which are related to a process called atherosclerosis." It goes on to describe that atherosclerosis is a condition involving plaque that can build up in arteries. The buildup makes it more difficult for blood to pass through the arteries, and clots can stop the flow altoghether. This causes what is known as a heart attack or stroke.$ ^1 $ But to make matters more confusing, the title of this page that describes "heart disease" is titled "cardiovascular-disease".

If you look at mortality statistics for North Carolina, nearly 19,000 people died due to a primary cause of heart disease in 2017.$2$ An additional 5,100 died from cerebrovascular disease (stroke). $3$ Heart disease is the 2nd leading cause of death for all individuals in North Carolina, and the top leading cause of death for residents 65 years of age or older (78% of all NC heart disease deaths).

While genetics play a role in the likelihood someone may be at risk of heart disease, serious outcomes may be prevented and/or mitigated through healthy lifestyle choices such as a balance diet and regular exercise.And so early detection is key to preventing the most serious of complications, including death.

The question I am exploring today is: If a classifier is developed to detect the presence of heart disease in a population, how does that same classifier perform on a younger population? Can the algorithm work equally as well as it does on a full age population on a sample of individuals younger than 50?

To do this analysis I will first use a classifier algorithm to predict if patient data means an individual has the presence of (any level) of heart disease, or no presence of heart disease. I will then run the algorithm on just the young portion of the test data, to see how the performance compares to the full population.

If differences appear, it could provide impetus for research into algorithms based on age of patient.

The dataset I selected for this exploration is the Heart Disease data set from the Center for Machine Learning and Intelligent Systems at the University of California, Irvine.$^5$ While rather dated (from 1990), the dataset is publicly available for use and pretty much preprocessed for use, making it a good dataset for learning and practicing my new skills.

The dataset is comprised of patient heart disease diagnosis (measured from 0 to 4), along with patient attributes and measurements. For the purposes of this study, I will classify either the presence of heart disease (1,2,3,or 4) or the absence of heart disease (0) as a binary classification.

The original dataset includes 76 measures, but only 14 measures are used in current published research. As such, these are the measures I plan to include in my classifier.
